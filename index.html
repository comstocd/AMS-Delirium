🔍 Differential Diagnosis Considerations

+

Delirium

  • Acute onset (hours-days)
  • Fluctuating course
  • Inattention prominent
  • Reversible cause present

Dementia

  • Chronic onset (months-years)
  • Stable course
  • Memory loss prominent
  • Progressive/irreversible

Depression

  • Weeks-months onset
  • Persistent low mood
  • Psychomotor slowing
  • "I don't know" answers

Psychosis

  • Organized delusions
  • Auditory hallucinations
  • Normal attention
  • Psychiatric history often present
Remember: Delirium can be superimposed on dementia. Obtain baseline cognitive function from family/caregivers. Up to 70% of elderly hospitalized patients have delirium + dementia.

Medication Review & Management

🛑 High-Risk Medications to HOLD/REDUCE

−
AGS Beers Criteria 2023: Avoid or reduce dose of deliriogenic medications whenever possible

Anticholinergics (HIGHEST RISK - STOP)

  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl), hydroxyzine
  • Promethazine (Phenergan)
  • Benztropine, trihexyphenidyl
  • First-generation antihistamines
  • Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
  • Dicyclomine, hyoscyamine, scopolamine

Benzodiazepines (STOP unless ETOH/benzo withdrawal)

  • Lorazepam (Ativan), alprazolam (Xanax)
  • Diazepam (Valium), clonazepam (Klonopin)
  • Temazepam, triazolam
  • Exception: CIWA protocol for alcohol withdrawal

Other High-Risk Medications (REDUCE/STOP)

  • Z-drugs: zolpidem (Ambien), eszopiclone (Lunesta)
  • H2 blockers: famotidine, ranitidine (prefer PPI)
  • Muscle relaxants: cyclobenzaprine, carisoprodol
  • Opioids: reduce to lowest effective dose
  • Antipsychotics: review continued need if on prior
  • Corticosteroids: can precipitate delirium, but don't stop abruptly

Management Plan

🎯 Non-Pharmacologic Interventions (FIRST LINE)

−
Evidence-based: Non-pharmacologic strategies are first-line per NICE CG103, SCCM Guidelines, AGS recommendations

Hospital HELP Protocol Elements:

  • Reorientation: Frequent verbal reorientation, visible clock/calendar, familiar objects from home
  • Sensory aids: Ensure glasses, hearing aids, dentures available and functioning
  • Sleep hygiene: Minimize nighttime interruptions, cluster care activities, reduce noise/light
  • Early mobilization: Out of bed to chair, ambulate TID, minimize bed rest
  • Adequate hydration: Encourage PO fluids, review IVF needs
  • Pain control: Scheduled acetaminophen, non-pharm methods, avoid opioids if possible
  • Eliminate tethers: Remove/minimize urinary catheters, telemetry, IV lines when safe
  • Family presence: Encourage family at bedside, familiar voices/photos
  • Minimize restraints: Use only as last resort for safety

💊 Pharmacologic Management (If Non-Pharm Insufficient)

−
⚠️ Black Box Warning: Antipsychotics increase mortality risk in elderly with dementia-related psychosis. Use lowest dose for shortest duration. Document informed decision-making.
Indications for Pharmacologic Treatment:
• Severe agitation threatening patient/staff safety
• Interfering with essential medical care (preventing intubation, pulling central lines)
• NOT indicated for: mild confusion, wandering, verbal agitation responding to redirection

Antipsychotic Options (consult pharmacy/local protocol):

  • Haloperidol: 0.5-1 mg IV/PO/IM q4-6h PRN
    • Pros: Fast-acting, IV option available
    • Cons: EPS risk, QTc prolongation
    • Contraindications: QTc >500ms, Parkinson's disease, Lewy body dementia
  • Quetiapine (Seroquel): 12.5-25 mg PO qHS or BID
    • Pros: Sedating, lower EPS risk, preferred in elderly
    • Cons: PO only, delayed onset, orthostasis
    • Often preferred as first-line in elderly patients
  • Risperidone: 0.25-0.5 mg PO BID
    • Pros: PO option, effective
    • Cons: Higher EPS risk than quetiapine
  • Olanzapine: 2.5-5 mg PO/IM daily
    • Pros: IM option available
    • Cons: Metabolic effects, sedation

AVOID Benzodiazepines (except specific indications):

  • Exception #1: Alcohol withdrawal (CIWA protocol with lorazepam)
  • Exception #2: Benzodiazepine withdrawal (taper protocol)
  • Exception #3: Seizures refractory to antiepileptics
  • Benzos worsen delirium in most cases - paradoxical agitation common in elderly
